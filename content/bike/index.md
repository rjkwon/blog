As of this writing I've been riding (homophone alert!!) this bike for about 13 years, which is the longest I've consistently ridden any single bike. I think (hope) it could last another 13 years. 

The process of putting it together was kind of slow and took about three years total. As with many useful skills, though, the struggle was kind of the point — I ended up with not only a rideable bike that I had built myself with my own two hands, which felt awesome, but also the ability to maintain 90% of it myself, since in the process I learned about every single component and how each works. I'm pretty proud of that! 

I also learned a lot more since then about what to do when things break or get worn out and how to fix and replace them. A lesson for life, perhaps.

Maybe in another world I would have been a bike mechanic?! I did try to be a people mechanic a.k.a. a general surgeon for the first part of my career and although the stakes of general surgery are ah not the same as the stakes of bike maintenance, they are otherwise not that dissimilar in terms of the actual work. Diagnosing the problem is often half the battle. Stuff needs to be repaired or removed or replaced. I remember finding out, live in the OR, that identifying the exact location of an intestinal perforation can be successfully achieved using the same technique as finding the hole in a punctured tube (methodically run it,  submerging each section in water and look for the bubbles), lol.

Anyway, I didn't mean to talk about intestines so much (classic kwon)... this page is _supposed_ to be about my bike. 

I don't ride as much as I used to when I was younger and had a higher appetite for risk (and didn't have the lower back of a middle aged woman), but I do take it out on the streets every once in awhile. There's still no other feeling like going down a smooth hill on a smooth bicycle on a warm night and feeling the breeze on your face.
